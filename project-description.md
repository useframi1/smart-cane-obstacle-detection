# Embedded Systems Course Project

## Overview

Your challenge is to design, build, debug, and demonstrate a complete embedded system that addresses a meaningful real-world problem or opportunity. This project is your primary opportunity in the course to build your **ASK (Applied Skills and Knowledge) portfolio**: a tangible demonstration of your capabilities in hardware-software co-design, system integration, debugging, teamwork, and engineering communication.

A strong project is not just a collection of connected parts. It should demonstrate thoughtful system design, clear requirements, effective implementation, careful testing, and professional documentation.

## Project Objectives

- Identify a real-world problem or opportunity that can be addressed using an embedded system
- Design a complete system, including the selection of appropriate microcontrollers, sensors, actuators, and supporting hardware
- Implement the system through hardware interfacing and robust embedded software development
- Test and debug the system to verify that it is reliable and satisfies its requirements
- Document the full design and implementation process clearly and professionally
- Communicate the project effectively through presentations and live demonstrations
- Collaborate productively within a small engineering team

## Team Formation

- Projects must be completed in teams of **three (3)** students.
- Students are responsible for forming their own teams.
- Team formation is the first required milestone.

## Project Scope

This is a student-driven project. Each team must propose its own idea.

To be approved, a project must:

- Be centered around one or more microcontrollers
- Interact with the physical world through sensors or other input devices
- Control actuators or output devices
- Solve a non-trivial, clearly defined problem
- Be realistic in scope for the available time
- Include meaningful engineering effort in both hardware and software

Projects that are too small, too vague, too dependent on copied tutorials, or too ambitious for the available time may be rejected or sent back for revision.

## Inspiration and Documentation Requirement

All teams are required to review the previous projects on the course GitHub wiki before finalizing their ideas. These previous projects should be used as a reference for expected scope, complexity, and documentation quality.

> Course wiki: [CSCE4301 Wiki](https://github.com/CSCE4301/wiki)

In addition, each team must create and maintain its own project page(s) on the same course wiki throughout the semester. Your wiki page is part of the project deliverables and must be updated continuously as the project evolves. It should not be written only at the end of the semester.

## Components and Sourcing

Teams are allowed to use components and materials available in the CSCE workshop, subject to availability and any workshop usage rules communicated during the semester.

Teams are also allowed to source additional components locally from Egyptian suppliers when needed. However, each team is responsible for checking part availability, compatibility, delivery time, and cost before committing to a design. Projects should be scoped around parts that can be sourced reliably within the project timeline.

Examples of local sources include:

- [RAM Electronics](https://ramelectronics.net/)
- [Future Electronics Egypt](https://www.futureelectronics.com/)
- [Most Electronics](https://mostelectronics.com/)
- [Micro Ohm](https://microohm.com/)
- [Makers Electronics](https://makerselectronics.com/)
- [Electra Store](https://electrastore.com/)

These are suggested sources only; teams are free to use other reliable suppliers as well.

## Project Approval Criteria

A proposal is more likely to be approved if it shows:

- A clear problem statement
- A practical and well-justified solution
- Appropriate technical ambition for a team of three
- A feasible hardware-software architecture
- Identifiable technical risks
- A realistic implementation plan

## Expectations and Good Practices

- Start simple and integrate early.
- Do not wait until the final week to connect hardware and software.
- Keep your wiki page updated continuously rather than writing it all at the end.
- Divide labor clearly, but make sure all team members understand the full system.
- Test individual modules early, then test the fully integrated system.
- Choose a project with enough ambition to be interesting, but not so much that it becomes unmanageable.
- Verify component availability early, especially if your project depends on specific sensors, motors, displays, communication modules, or power components.

---

## Final Note

A successful project is one that is well-scoped, functional, clearly explained, and well documented. Ambition is good, but disciplined execution matters more.
